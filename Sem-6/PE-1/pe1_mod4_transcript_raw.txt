Module 4 Overview
Overview
How has the digital revolution altered the way products are priced? In this module, you
will learn how new digital tools are enabling customers to take a more active role in
setting the prices they pay for the products they buy. After introducing the notion of
price and how this aspect of the marketing mix is changing, we will explore how University
of Illinois students use digital tools to obtain lower prices for the things they buy. We will
then examine emerging digital concepts: Pay What You Want (PWYW) and Freemium
pricing strategies.
In addition to learning about these concepts, you will also have the opportunity to
apply them through a hands-on exercise (It's Full of Sparks) [Honors Assignment] and a
real-world case study (Panera). As a result of these various learning activities, you will
develop a deep understanding of how digital tools are transforming the pricing landscape.
Goals and Objectives
 Understand the pricing aspect of the marketing mix.
 Appreciate how digital tools are changing how products are priced.
 Learn about the Freemium model and how this pricing strategy works.
 Learn what Pay What You Want (PWYW) is and how this pricing strategy works.
 Analyze a real-world case that employs a PWYW pricing strategy.
 Engage in a hands-on exercise in which you can experience the Freemium model
[Honors Assignment].
Guiding Questions
 What is the Pay What You Want (PWYW) pricing strategy, and how does it work?
 What is the Freemium model, and how does it work?
 How are new digital tools changing the way rms price their products and how
consumers evaluate prices?

A pricing strategy is a rm's basic approach to how it prices its products, rms can

Competitor-based pricing is a strategy in which the price of a product is based upon

Speaker: Steve
Pricing is the moment of truth. All marketing comes into focus in the pricing deci-

As illustrated by this quote by pricing expert Thomas Nagle, picking the right price
is an important part of a rm's marketing strategy. If the price is too high, rms will
lose potential sales. If it's too low, they will lose potential revenue. Thus, the key is to
nd the price that's just right.
The problem is, what is right for you, maybe too high or too low for me. Thus,
allowing price to vary according to the value that a product provides to various potential
customers makes a lot of sense.
Firms try to do this in a number of ways. For example, in the US, movie theaters oer
discounts to demographic groups that traditionally have lower incomes, such as students
and senior citizens. This approach is called price segmentation, and is commonly used as
a pricing approach. The setting of these dierent price segments have been traditionally
determined by rms rather than by customers. For example, my local grocery store oers
discounts on donuts that are two days old. I can't walk into the store and say that I
wanna pay a discounted price for freshly baked donuts. That's not how it works.
However, in recent years, a few rms have experimented with the idea of allowing
their customers to segment themselves by letting them name their own price. This rather
unorthodox strategy is gaining popularity, and is called pay what you want, or PWYW
for short. Although this pricing strategy has been employed in physical stores, it is
ideally suited for digital marketing because of the increased ability of rms to control the
distribution of their product and to track customer payment activity. In addition, since
the marginal cost of a digital good is close to zero, the strategy is relatively low risk.
A wide variety of rms have experimented with a PWYW strategy, including.
1. Radiohead. Back in 2007, the British rock band Radiohead oered its new album
in Rainbow for sale using a PWYW strategy. This approach received worldwide
attention and was a launching pad for the Pay What You Want movement. We'll

3.2 Denition of Pay What You Want
Pay What You Want is a pricing strategy that lets customers decide how much they
wanna pay for a particular product. Although a rm may suggest a price, its customers
are free to pay less or more than this price. Indeed, several pay-as-you-want oerings let
customers pay even nothing.
3.3 Deeper Dive: Three Key Issues
There are a lot of interesting issues about Pay What You Want. In this lecture, I will
focus on three specic issues.
1. Do people actually pay when they don't have to?
According to standard economic thought, customers are seeking to maximize their utility.
In other words, they wanna get as much as they can for as little as they have to pay.
Thus, from a strictly economic perspective, a Pay What You Want strategy seems quite
foolish, as rational economic actors would just take a product without paying anything
for it.
However, in reality, many people are not just self-interested economic actors, but also
social well-beings who are governed by the norms of honesty and fairness. Thus, while
some do behave as economists suggest, many don't. For example, an academic study
on the buying behavior of customers of a Pay What You Want Restaurant in Vienna,
Austria found that over 99% paid something for the food they ate.

2. How can a rm make money by giving its product away for
free?
This is a really good question. A rm can't make money or stay in business if it continu-

product even under a pay what you want strategy. Indeed, as we see it in the Radiohead

Also, because a pay what you want strategy is novel and newsworthy, when a rm

pay what you want on a rather limited basis. For example, Radiohead used Pay What
You Want during the release of one of its recent albums, but employed a more traditional
pricing approach for its next album release. Thus, Pay What You Want appears to be
an approach that is a good change of pace, but not sustainable as a long-term pricing
strategy.

4.2 Four Practical Recommendations for PWYW
Speaker: Steve
Now that you've learned a little bit about what pay what you want is and how it
works, here are four recommendations for putting this concept into practice.
1. Set a Reference Price
As we discussed earlier, most customers have a reference price in mind when evaluating
a potential purchase. This reference price eect can also be employed in pay what you
want pricing. One way of doing this would be to clearly display the price of a comparative
item. For example, if a rm oers pay what you want pricing for vitamin C, it could
announce this next to its price for vitamin D. Most people wanna think of themselves as
fair and honest. Thus, the setting of a reference price should decrease the temptation to
pay little or nothing for a product that employs a pay what you want strategy.
2. Focus on Marginal Costs
Although most customers will likely pay something even when they can name their own
price, this is a risky strategy. Thus, pay what you want is best employed for products
that have low marginal cost. So pay what you want is especially attractive for digital
goods such as music, text, and video. For these type of products, each additional unit
has a very low marginal cost. A rm can take this even one step further by pairing up
a digital pay what you want product with a physical product that has a relatively high
price listed. For example, as part of its pay what you want strategy, Radiohead also
oered customers the opportunity to buy a limited edition discbox of its music for $82.
It sold 100,000 of these disc boxes for a total of over $8 million in revenue.

3. Try a Limited Rollout
As discussed earlier, most successful pay what you want strategies are applied to a lim-
